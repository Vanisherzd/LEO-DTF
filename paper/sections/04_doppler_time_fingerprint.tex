\section{Doppler-Time Fingerprint}\label{sec:dtf}

\subsection{Fingerprint as a Geometric Signature}
The Doppler-time fingerprint is the functional relationship
$f_D(t; \mathbf{x})$ induced by a candidate position $\mathbf{x}$ over the
duration of a satellite pass.  Because the range-rate $\dot{\rho}(t; \mathbf{x})$
is geometry-dependent, two distinct positions inside the ROI generally produce
different Doppler trajectories.  The \emph{shape} of this trajectory across the
pass---its curvature, peak value, and zero-crossing time---constitutes a
position-dependent fingerprint.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Nuisance Parameters}
The observed frequency $y_f(t_i)$ adds to $f_D$ a vertical offset $b_0$, a linear
tilt $b_1 t_i$, and noise $\epsilon_i$.  The timing offset $\Delta t$ shifts the
propagation delay and indirectly affects the Doppler curve through $\rho(t)$.
These four nuisance terms must be separated from the geometric component to
recover position information.

\begin{itemize}
  \item $b_0$: shifts the entire frequency curve vertically.
  \item $b_1$: tilts the curve; positive drift makes later observations
        systematically higher.
  \item $\Delta t$: shifts the delay observation; has secondary effect on
        Doppler through the range at each time.
\end{itemize}

The estimator handles these by joint optimization over position and nuisance
parameters (Section~\ref{sec:estimator}).

\subsection{Discriminative Power of Multi-Time Observations}
A single snapshot observation provides at most one constraint (range-rate) on
two unknown position coordinates, making the problem underdetermined.  Tracking
the Doppler curve over a 5--10 minute pass samples the geometry at multiple
elevation angles, substantially increasing the position information content.
This motivates the grid-search approach rather than a closed-form solution.
